Summary: Australia is about to introduce new laws that will change how political donations work and our foreign relationships



The government and Labour party have worked together to push new laws to prevent espionage. As both major parties are working on it, it is likely to go pass.



By the way, when opposing sides agree in Parliament, it is called bipartisan.



This is a big change so let’s break this down into two main points, the effects on foreign donations and the effect on individuals.



But first, some definitions.



Espionage: The practice of spying or of using spies, to obtain political and military information



First on the agenda, these new laws are aiming to completely ban foreign donations for politics. This covers everything from donations to major parties to donations to politically focussed charities.



Turnbull hopes that this will help reduce espionage and collusion with foreign powers.

The definition of the criminal offence of espionage will be changed to include any possession of confidential documents.



And also, if the laws goes through, Australia will implement a system where all foreign citizens in Australia who have links with other foreign powers must register themselves on a list.

Laws to stop harassing women entering abortion clinics (and implement common sense)

On Thursday, after a few hours of discussion, the NSW government passed a law to stop the harassing of women that enter abortion clinics, and joins the ACT, Victoria, Tasmania and the Northern Territory in similar regulations.



The law essentially establishes an area of 150 meters around abortion clinics for safe zones and even threatens with potential jail time if people are still found doing this.



During the discussions on Thursday night, there were two definite opposite sides. Nationals and Labor MPs discussed the law, with Labor MP Leslie Williams saying that it was “not about abortions” but “respecting the rights of others.”
