The Aftermath

By: Alexander W


Summary: I talk about some of the implications of the leadership spill.



Well readers, all has been said and done and we have been left with a new Government. Scott Morrison is our Prime Minister, and a few changes have been made to the Cabinet (which you can read about here).



We have a few newcomers to the Cabinet, such as Melissa Price and Karen Andrews, who have jumped into the spotlight after the leadership spill. No doubt that they were promised something by Scott Morrison for their political support.



Interestingly, Peter Dutton remains in the Cabinet, as the Minister for Home Affairs (minus Immigration). Scott Morrison seems to be trying to rebuild the Liberal Party team after this damaging spill.



At the end of the day, I think that this spill has been extremely unproductive and almost offensive considering the political context.



While NSW was in a drought crisis and important international decisions - such as the Indonesian trade talks - were to be made, our politicians were bickering in a power struggle.



If our Government isn’t there for us in times of need, what’s their point? This has already happened many times now. Perhaps it is time to implement party rules similar to the ones described by Kevin Rudd.



Ok. It’s easy to criticize but what are the actual benefits of this reshuffle? To my mind, they are few and far between.



I can concede that perhaps the old Coalition wasn’t representing the views of the conservative Ministers and their constituents. If this was the case, then changes must be made to ensure proper representation.



However, the new Cabinet does not seem to be any more conservative than the previous, with many previous Ministers retaining their roles.



And maybe, we’ll have another reshuffle again soon if the Coalition loses the election.



Then again, if this can solve some of the issues within the Coalition and lead to a more cohesive Government then perhaps the lost time will be worth it.



Let’s be honest, as seen in the polls (which “showed Labor leading the coalition 56 per cent to the Government's 44 per cent on a two-party preferred basis.” - ABC), unless something drastic happens, Bill Shorten and the Labor Party are going to take over anyway.



And look, maybe that’s why we had this spill. To try and breathe a few gasps of life into a suffocation Government. After all, this recent incident has blasted Scott Morrison into the media and given him more attention, even more than everybody's favourite American President.

Trump Cancels Visit to Australia.

By: Zack N

Summary: Donald Trump is now unlikely to visit Australia this year.



Donald Trump had planned to visit the Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation Summit in Papua New Guinea later this year.



He was also planning to visit Australia after this summit. It would have been his first visit to Australia since becoming president and his first chance to meet our new prime minister.



Recently, news from the white house has showed that he is pulling out of the summit and will instead send Mike Pence, the vice president. He will be instead in France for the 100th anniversary of end of the First World War.



This is a shame for Australia as it would have been a good chance to reaffirm a relationship with a longstanding ally. As we’ve seen, Donald Trump can be very unpredictable.



Scott Morrison spoke with Donald Trump on the phone last week and had what he called a “great discussion”.



With his recent visit to Indonesia and his progress on an Indonesia-Australia free trade deal, ScoMo’s international relationships are not looking too bad, and this would only have added to that.

Julie Bishop, WAsted.

By: Alexander W

Summary: Although Julie Bishop has probably lost the most from the last leadership spill, she has expressed determination in continuing her political career.



The curious case of Julie Bishop can be compared to the tragedy of Julius Caesar. A popular and loved politician attempts to elevate their career and gets backstabbed by those whom they thought they could trust.



In Caesar’s case, he tried to use his popularity amongst the common people to become Emperor of Rome. This lead to him being murdered by other senators and even those he trusted, as Shakespeare puts it; “Et tu Brute?”



Photo: Ok, maybe it wasn't this dramatic. Source

And at a glance, Julie Bishop seems similar. An influential and loved politician attempts to become Prime Minister, only to be deserted by all her fellow WA MPs in favour of ScoMo. Similar to Caesar’s death, it seemed like this was the death of her career.



However, “the Liberal's most popular federal member in WA” (Peter Kennedy) will run again in the next election for the electorate of Curtin (which she won by 70% last election).



And, more power to her! Parliament needs more influential women (and MP’s for that matter) just like her.
