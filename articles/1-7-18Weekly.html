Company Tax Cuts has the Labor party cut-up

By: Alexander W


Summary: Bill Shorten and the Labor party have backtracked on their promise to completely oppose the Company Tax Cut Bill.



Earlier this week, Shorten and the Labor party seemed very determined to repeal Company Tax Cuts. He claimed that tax cuts for medium sized businesses ($10 million - $50 million turnover) would be repealed if elected as Government.



Photo: Bill Shorten’s political life shortens with this incident. Source from here.

Well, turns out that Shorten has a big mouth. The Labor party actually don’t want to repeal that Tax Cut.



On Thursday, Shorten, this time with his fellow ministers watching over him very closely, stated that there was some there was a mix-up within the party’s policy and the Labor party will keep the Tax Cuts.



Hmmmm... Seems suspicious to me.



Shorten claimed that this mistake was due to “confusion” and “uncertainty” within the party.

Photo: Phew… It’s getting hot in here. Source fromhere.


The only thing “confusing” about this situation is the complete lack of communication and coordination in the Labor party room. How could they let a mistake this big happen? And the only thing “uncertain” about this situation is Labor’s chance at winning the election after this major screw-up.



This situation has led to one of the greatest political roasts we’ve seen for a while.



PM Malcolm Turnbull called the situation



“completely and utterly humiliating”



And he criticised Shorten by saying that Shorten:



"He can't keep the same policy position for a week"



In our opinion, this criticism is completely fair. Communication and diplomacy are basic skills that all politicians should be practicing.



And this week’s incident just goes to show the lack of both of these skills within the Labor party room and with their leader.

Penalty Rate Cuts

By: Gautam M

Summary: Government introduces new penalty rate cuts to fund the great tax cuts brought by the budget.



From the 1st of July 2018 the Australian government will be cutting penalty rates for workers (the extra pay that you get for working overtime or on holidays and stuff like that) in certain industries. Those being the hospitality, fast-food, pharmacy and retail industries.



Figure: Reduced penalty rates don’t look good. Source from here.

You know what the funny thing is though? Politicians get to enjoy a pay rise of 2% from Sunday, basically giving someone like Turnbull an extra $10,500 a year . That makes him earn just over $538,000 a year. .



The aim behind the changes is to help fund the tax cuts mentioned at this year’s budget.



On the other hand, as directed by a Fair Work Commission Ruling about a month ago, the minimum work wage will go up 3.5% as of the 1st of July, to $18.93 an hour.



Bill Shorten talked about the reduction of penalty rates over his time at the NSW Labor Party conference, and basically said that he will restore the original penalty rates if he’s voted in.



Although, given his backflip on the Company tax cuts, we aren’t so sure that he will stick to his word.



Sources: http://www.abc.net.au/news/2018-06-29/sunday-penalty-rate-changes-take-effect-this-weekend/9924344?section=politics



National Redress Scheme

By: Alexander W

Summary: The Government has accepted a recommendation from the Royal Commission into Child Sexual Abuse to roll out a National Redress Scheme.



For context, over the past few years, a Royal Commission was established to investigate cases of Child Sexual Abuse in national institutions.



A few weeks ago, the Government accepted 104 of the Commission’s 122 recommendations, including a National Redress (admitting fault and compensating) Scheme.



This Sunday, the National Redress Scheme will roll out, which essentially allows victims of sexual abuse who were in National Institutes to receive up to a maximum of $150,000 in compensation, although the average payment will be around $76,000.



While there are some flaws in this system, overall it is a positive step. This scheme acknowledges the harms that were done to these people and that they deserve an apology. Furthermore, it gives victims compensation without forcing them to go to court.



The main piece of criticism that has been raised is to do with how the Government will determine payment amounts. Given that for many people this is a sensitive matter, how will the Government determine how much each case is “worth”?



This could lead to really awkward situations where the government finds that someone’s case isn’t worth anything or perhaps very little.
