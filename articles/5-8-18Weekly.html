The Big Dry - Drought in NSW and Queensland

By: Alexander W


Summary: Farmers have been going through a bad drought for the past month in July.



The almost customary Australian drought has hit us again.



Last month, while we were freezing our butts off in the ACT, much of Queensland experienced a record-breakingly hot July.




While it sounds pleasant from my perspective, the heat has caused a severe drought in Queensland and some of NSW. The intense heat has lead to the evaporation of moisture in the soil, meaning plants cannot grow.



Currently, many farmers are struggling to afford or even buy feed for their livestock, as hay is in short supply across the country.



And in most cases, it seems like a lose-lose situation, as the hay used to feed livestock costs more than the livestock is worth.



As well as the financial effects, droughts have great social costs for both children and adults.



In an interview with the ABC, Manilla Central School principal Michael Windred said



"There's the emotional drain on our students, it's the lack of sleep, it's the constant worry, it's got to have a bearing on their studying”



Many students in families affected by the drought are getting up at the break of dawn to give an extra helping hand on their farms.

On the other hand, adults are feeling the stress of coping with dying livestock and an unstable future.

Even in these tough times, Australians across the country have pitched in to help out, with many donations and aid program(such as this creative initiative about donating formal dresses for drought-affected school students) being developed.

Benfold’s, the adopted, aborted, stepbrother of the Australian Penfold’s

By: Alexander W

Summary: A new online shopping company called Pinduoduo has been caught red-handed for selling, or at least facilitating the sale, of fake Australian wines.



If our previous story made you thirsty, prepare to be quenched.



In the recent few years, Pinduoduo has risen to become one of the largest companies in China. It was recently admitted onto the Nasdaq (the American stock exchange) with a value of $USD 24 million.



Perhaps one could say that this has caused the Chinese giant to become “drunk” with power as they have recently been caught selling many fake products, including Australian wines.



Photo: Benfold’s claims that it is a “legitimate” company and totally separate from Penfold’s. Source

Most notably, Penfold’s and Yellow Tail have had many rip-offs and fakes released. For Penfold’s, the Chinese rip-off “Benfold’s” and for Yellow Tail, it’s adopted Chinese sibling, “Coastel Pearl”.



To most Australians and English speakers, they can be clearly distinguished from the original products, as the labels are often misspelt or use incorrect grammar.



However, the rate at which these fakes are selling in China has major impacts on Australian wines.



The ABC found that “One merchant on the platform alone had sold more than 1,000 six-bottle cases of their "Benford’s Hyland" wine at $117 a case”.



This will directly result in fewer sales of legitimate Australian wines as well as the depreciation of our wine’s reputation - excluding goon of course, as goon doesn’t have a good reputation in the first place.



The economic and political implications of these wine fakes could worsen an already shaky relationship with China.

Values of Taxi Licenses Continue to Fall

By: Zack N

Summary: As the popularity of ride sharing grows, the Taxi industry is dying out, destroying people’s livelihoods.



Next time you catch an Uber, think about this.



A Taxi licence in New York used to cost over $1 million. Now they can be bought for less than $150,000.



The purpose of these licenses was to limit the number of Taxis on the road. This would push up fares and ensure that drivers were able to earn a living, rather than being forced to drive and earn barely enough to live.



As ride sharing services such as Uber and Lyft grow in popularity, many drivers turn to these companies because of the cheaper upfront cost and greater number of customers,, however, they end up paying the price in the long run because of systems that these companies use to avoid paying even minimum wages (if your interested in this, check out the second on our list of sources).



This doesn’t only have an economic effect, six New York city drivers have committed suicide since last December.



Additionally, people have invested in these licences as a means of securing retirement funds, only to find out that they can’t even sell them for a fraction of the price they bought them for.



This problem exists in Australia too, and is a real problem for politicians trying to regulate the situation. They have to try and balance between allowing Uber to operate and provide convenience for citizens, while compensating people involved in the taxi industry.



The Victorian Labour government has given out $500 million to people affected by the change while the liberals say they would give more if they were in power. The question is, is this enough?



We’ll let you decide next time you need to get from A to B.
